Part I

How we found it.

01 — The background

First, what an AI agent's “memory” actually is.

To understand the problem, it helps to know one new thing about how modern assistants work.

A few years ago, a chatbot forgot everything the moment a conversation ended. Today's AI agents do not. After each session they write notes to themselves — a plain-text file, often literally called MEMORY.md, that the model itself authors, re-reads at the start of every future conversation, and rewrites as it learns more. It is the agent's private diary. Whatever ends up in it silently shapes every decision the agent makes for you afterwards.

The quiet assumption everyone makes is simple: what the agent writes in that diary is what you actually told it. Our research is about a specific, surprising way that assumption breaks — not by the agent forgetting things, but by the agent adding things you never said, and recording them as fact.

Why study this now? Persistent memory is the fastest-growing and least-scrutinised piece of agent infrastructure in 2026 (see Park et al. 2023; Packer et al. 2023). The benchmarks that exist measure whether agents remember enough — almost none ask whether what they write is true to the source.

02 — The dead end that pointed the way

We started on a different problem — and it failed.

Every result here is the survivor of a project that first hit a wall. The wall is what told us where to look.

Our original idea, which we nicknamed “Soul Drift,” was that an agent's memory slowly distorts each time it is re-summarised — like a photocopy of a photocopy. In small and mid-tier models, it did. But the moment we ran the identical test on a frontier model (DeepSeek V4-pro), the drift was flat zero. The effect got weaker as models got stronger.

That is fatal for a paper. A weakness that shrinks as models improve is a weakness that is already solving itself — a reviewer would rightly ask why anyone should care. So we inverted the entire question, and that inversion is the whole insight:

Stop looking for a bug that capability cures.
Find the bug that capability causes.

The most important failures in AI safety — deception, scheming, over-reasoning — are exactly the ones that require intelligence to commit. A weak model cannot scheme; it is not capable enough. If a memory failure worked the same way, it would survive at the frontier by definition. So we went hunting for one.

03 — The hunt, and the “aha”

A capable model reasons more. What if it reasons into its memory?

We ran a large automated literature search — a fan-out of agents reading roughly 190 papers across ACL, NeurIPS, and ICLR — looking for a capability-dependent memory failure that nobody had measured. One candidate survived every check, and it matched the inversion perfectly.

Here is the intuition. Faced with a vague, messy human message, a capable model does what it was trained to do: infer, fill gaps, smooth things over. In a live reply, that is helpful. But when the model is writing its own memory, those same inferences get committed to the diary as if they were facts the user stated. A smarter model infers more — so it should corrupt its memory more. We named it provenance collapse: the line between what was said and what was guessed collapses inside the agent's own notes.

Part II

What it is.

04 — The phenomenon in one figure

Two true notes, then one invented one.

The whole failure, as a single diagram — the kind of figure a paper opens with.

Figure 1 Provenance collapse. (a) The user makes a brief, ordinary remark. (b) The agent consolidates it into memory, reasoning as it goes. (c) Two faithful notes are written — then a third, an unsupported inference, is recorded as durable fact. Nothing in the session said the pipeline is “unreliable.” That invented claim now conditions every future session.
05 — The two ideas you need

Commission, not omission.

Omission is forgetting — losing a fact that was true. Every existing memory benchmark measures this, and small models do it more. Commission is the opposite: writing down a fact that was never true. Provenance collapse is a commission failure — and we expected (correctly) that capable models do it more.

How we count it. We break each memory note into individual claims, and check every claim against the original conversation. A claim is a collapse when it is (1) not supported by anything the user said, and (2) written without any hedge — stated as flat fact rather than “maybe” or “seems.” The model's collapse rate is simply the fraction of its claims that fail this test.

06 — Why it matters, in real life

Three ordinary moments where it goes wrong.

The lab example is abstract. Here is what provenance collapse looks like in deployments people actually use — the user's words on top, what the agent files away below, with the invented part marked.

① Personal / work assistant
USER SAID — “Ugh, my manager pushed our 1:1 to next week again.”
AGENT RECORDED — The user has a strained relationship with their manager and feels disrespected and undervalued.never stated
Why it's dangerous: the next time the user asks for help drafting a message to that manager, the assistant quietly writes in a defensive, resentful tone — acting on a relationship problem that exists only in its own memory.
② Clinical / health intake assistant
PATIENT SAID — “I sometimes miss my evening tablet when I work late shifts.”
AGENT RECORDED — Patient is non-adherent to their medication regimen.never stated
Why it's dangerous: “non-adherent” is a loaded clinical label that can change how a patient is triaged and treated. The patient described an occasional, situational lapse — the agent recorded a standing diagnosis.
③ Customer-support agent
CUSTOMER SAID — “This is the second time I'm asking about the refund.”
AGENT RECORDED — Customer is highly frustrated and at serious risk of churning.never stated
Why it's dangerous: that single invented line can route the customer into a different (costlier, or more defensive) treatment track on every future contact — a consequential business decision built on a guess.

The common thread: in all three, the user said something narrow and factual; the agent recorded something broad, evaluative, and unstated — then treated it as ground truth forever after. That is the entire failure, and it compounds with every session.

Part IV

What we found.

09 — The headline result

The smarter the model, the more it invents.

A small base model collapses on 7% of its claims. Every larger, frontier, or reasoning model — across five vendors — lands at 22–40%.

Figure 4 · Collapse rate by model

small base modellarger / frontier / reasoning
Share of self-authored claims that are unsupported inferences stated as fact. Every model above the anchor differs at Fisher one-sided p ≤ 0.0003. Hover for counts.

It holds across five independent vendors — Meta, OpenAI, Mistral, DeepSeek, Qwen, Zhipu. Not one lab's quirk.

It persists at the very frontier. GPT-5 (29.8%) and DeepSeek V4-pro (30.1%) are among the worst — the models people trust most. Capability does not cure it; this is the result that the failed “Soul Drift” project could never have produced.

Stated honestly, it is a threshold. The jump is from “small base model” to “everything capable”; within a single family the climb is directional but not strictly step-by-step. We therefore describe it as a capability threshold, not a smooth dial — and say so plainly in the paper.

10 — The mechanism, visible

Give it a gap, and it fills it.

Inside every model, collapse is near-zero when the user states things plainly, and rises sharply when the input is vague — the signature of gap-filling.

Figure 5 · Collapse rate by input ambiguity

Three representative models. When users state facts outright, almost no collapse; when input is vague and emotional, capable models reach 40–56%. The effect tracks how much there is to infer.

Part V

How to stop it.

12 — The fix

One paragraph of instruction nearly erases it.

A “provenance-disciplined” prompt — record only what was stated; mark inferences as inferred; omit when unsure — cuts collapse by 60–97%.

Figure 6 · Baseline vs. provenance-disciplined prompt

Paired on identical sessions. Reductions significant at McNemar p ≤ 0.0034 for all three models. Dark = baseline, light = disciplined.

“But did it just write less?”

A fair worry: maybe it cut inventions only by writing fewer notes — losing real facts too. So we measured stated-fact recall: of what the user genuinely said, how much survives?

Figure 7 · Stated-fact recall — baseline vs disciplined

Recall stays within ~3 points (every paired-difference confidence interval includes zero) while collapse drops 60–97%. The fix removes inventions, not facts. The confound is closed.

Part VII

The paper.

The working ACL submission in its own format — abstract, numbered sections, a results table, and figure references.

Inference as Fact: Capability-Scaled Provenance Collapse
in Self-Authored Agent Memory
VelaOps Research
working draft · target: ACL 2027 (main)
Abstract. LLM agents increasingly maintain their own persistent natural-language memory — notes the model writes to itself and re-reads across sessions. We identify and measure a write-time failure we call provenance collapse: the agent records unsupported inferences as flat declarative facts the user never stated — a commission error, distinct from the omission/recall failures existing memory benchmarks measure. Across 30 controlled sessions and 10 models from 5 vendors, provenance collapse scales with model capability: a small base model (Llama-4-17B) collapses on 7.1% of self-authored claims, while every larger, frontier, or reasoning model — including GPT-5 and DeepSeek-V4-pro — collapses on 22–40% (all Fisher one-sided p ≤ 0.0003). It is a capability threshold that persists at the frontier and grows with input ambiguity. Our metric is validated by cross-family judge agreement (κ = 0.63–0.68). The failure is largely fixable at the prompt level: a provenance-disciplined instruction reduces collapse 60–97% (McNemar p ≤ 0.0034) while preserving stated-fact recall.

1Introduction

Persistent memory is becoming standard agent infrastructure (Park et al., 2023; Packer et al., 2023): assistants summarise each session into durable notes that condition future behaviour. The implicit assumption is that what the agent writes is what it was told. We show this fails systematically, and worse in more capable models. We define provenance collapse as writing, into memory, a claim not entailed by the session, as if stated fact (Figure 1). Contributions: (i) the phenomenon and a validated metric; (ii) a capability-scaling law across 10 models and 5 vendors; (iii) a prompt-level mitigation.

2Related Work

Concurrent work (Jin et al., 2026) names “provenance-role collapse” and proposes a typed-memory architecture, but reports no baseline rate and no capability scaling. Memory benchmarks — LoCoMo (Maharana et al., 2024), LongMemEval (Wu et al., 2025) — measure recall, not write-time fabrication. PrefEval (2025) measures adherence to stated preferences, the converse. Inverse scaling (McKenzie et al., 2023) and TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022) document larger-is-worse on prompted QA truthfulness; CoT unfaithfulness (Turpin et al., 2023) and sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2024) concern reply-level behaviour, not persistent self-authored artifacts.

3Method

We construct 30 sessions at three ambiguity levels. Each model consolidates each session into memory; a held-out judge from a different family decomposes each note into atomic claims and labels them entailed / unsupported / contradicted (Figure 3). Collapse rate = unsupported-and-unhedged ÷ total claims. Controls: ≥30 claims/cell, paired tests, prompt-paraphrase arm, cross-family judge (κ 0.63–0.68).

4Results

Collapse rises from 7.1% (Llama-4-17B) to 22–40% across nine larger/frontier/reasoning models, every contrast Fisher p ≤ 0.0003 (Table 1, Figure 4). It persists at the frontier (GPT-5 29.8%, V4-pro 30.1%) and grows with ambiguity (Figure 5): Qwen3-32B rises 8.9% → 44.1% → 55.9% from low to high.

5Mitigation

A provenance-disciplined consolidation prompt cuts collapse 60–97% (Qwen3-32B 36.1→1.0%, Llama-3.3-70B 24.7→4.7%, GPT-5-mini 16.5→6.5%; McNemar p ≤ 0.0034; Figure 6). Stated-fact recall is preserved within 3 points (Figure 7), ruling out a “wrote-less” confound.

6Limitations

Absolute magnitude is prompt-sensitive; the capability ordering is robust but within-family steps are not individually significant (a threshold, not a smooth law). Labels are judge-based (human validation in progress). Sessions are synthetic; naturalistic deployment is future work.

7Conclusion

Provenance collapse is, to our knowledge, the first LLM-agent memory failure shown to intensify with capability rather than fade — measurable, cross-vendor, present at the frontier, and substantially fixable with a single prompt.

15 — What we're not claiming

The honest fine print.

Magnitude is prompt-sensitive. Absolute rates shift with consolidation wording (one model fell to 2.4% under one phrasing). The capability ordering holds; we report per-prompt.
A threshold, not a smooth law. Within-family frontier steps are directional but not individually significant.
Judge-based labels. Validated by cross-family agreement (κ 0.63–0.68); gold human labels still being collected.
Controlled, synthetic sessions. 30 hand-built transcripts; naturalistic long-horizon deployments are next.
16 — Why it matters
As we hand agents durable memory and more capability, they don't just remember better — they remember things we never said, and write them down as truth.

Provenance collapse is the first LLM-agent memory failure shown to intensify with capability rather than fade. Measurable, cross-vendor, present at the frontier — and substantially fixable with a single prompt. A novel phenomenon, a validated metric, a capability law, and a working mitigation.
