Provenance CollapseVelaOps Research · 2026
A failure that grows as models get smarter

Inference as Fact: when an AI agent writes its own memory, it writes down things you never said.

A smart assistant keeps a private notebook about you. We found that the smarter it is, the more often it fills that notebook with guesses — recorded as facts. Here is the whole story, in plain words.

VelaOps Research · prepared for academic review · June 2026 · target venue ACL 2027

A few years ago, a chatbot forgot everything the moment you closed the tab. Today's AI agents do not. After each conversation they write notes to themselves — a small text file, often literally named MEMORY.md — that the model writes, re-reads at the start of every future chat, and rewrites as it goes. It is the agent's private diary, and whatever ends up in it quietly shapes every later decision it makes for you.

Everyone makes one quiet assumption about that diary: what the agent writes in it is what you actually told it. This page is about a specific, surprising way that assumption breaks. Not by the agent forgetting things — but by the agent adding things you never said, and writing them down as fact.

Here is the whole idea in one line. You say:

“the deploy failed again last night.”
…and the agent remembers: “the user's infrastructure is chronically unreliable.”

That second sentence is a reasonable guess. But it was written into permanent memory as a plain fact, and the user never said it. We call this provenance collapse — the line between what was said and what was guessed collapses inside the agent's own notes. The rest of this page explains how we found it, how we measured it, and a simple way to stop it.

Where this came from

Every result here is the survivor of a project that first failed — and the failure is what told us where to look.

Our first idea was simple. We thought an agent's memory slowly changes meaning each time the model rewrites it, losing a little more on every pass. In small and mid-size models, this happened. Then we ran the same test on a top model, DeepSeek V4-pro. The change was zero. The problem got smaller as the model got better.

That is fatal for a research paper. A weakness that shrinks as models improve is one that is already fixing itself — a reviewer would rightly ask why it matters. So we flipped the question, and that flip is the whole insight:

Stop looking for a bug that capability cures.
Find the bug that capability causes.

The most important failures in AI safety — deception, scheming, over-confidence — are the ones that require intelligence to commit. A weak model cannot scheme; it is not capable enough. If a memory failure worked the same way, it would survive at the frontier by definition. A large literature search pointed us to one candidate, and the intuition was this: a capable model, handed a vague human message, does what it was trained to do — infer, and fill gaps. When it does that while writing its own memory, the inference gets saved as fact. A smarter model infers more, so it should corrupt its memory more.

Two ideas to keep in mind

Commission, not omission. Omission is forgetting — losing a fact that was true. Every existing memory benchmark measures this, and small models do it more. Commission is the opposite: writing down a fact that was never true. Provenance collapse is a commission failure, and we expected capable models to do it more.

How we count it. We break each memory note into separate claims and check every claim against the original conversation. A claim is a collapse when it is (1) not supported by anything the user said, and (2) written with no hedge — as a flat fact, not a “maybe.” A model's collapse rate is just the share of its claims that fail this test.

Five everyday examples

The lab example is abstract. Here is what provenance collapse looks like in tools people actually use — the user's words first, what the agent files away next, with the invented part marked.

① Personal / work assistant
USER — “Ugh, my manager pushed our 1:1 to next week again.”
AGENT WROTE — The user has a strained relationship with their manager and feels disrespected.never said
Why it's dangerous: next time the user asks for help writing to that manager, the assistant quietly adopts a defensive tone — acting on a conflict that exists only in its own memory.
② Clinical / health intake assistant
PATIENT — “I sometimes miss my evening tablet when I work late shifts.”
AGENT WROTE — Patient is non-adherent to their medication regimen.never said
Why it's dangerous: “non-adherent” is a loaded clinical label that can change how a patient is treated. The patient described an occasional lapse; the agent recorded a standing diagnosis.
③ Customer-support agent
CUSTOMER — “This is the second time I'm asking about the refund.”
AGENT WROTE — Customer is highly frustrated and at serious risk of churning.never said
Why it's dangerous: that one invented line can send the customer down a different, costlier path on every future contact — a real business decision built on a guess.
④ Study / tutoring assistant
STUDENT — “I didn't finish the calculus problem set this week.”
AGENT WROTE — The student struggles with calculus and is falling behind in math.never said
Why it's dangerous: the tutor starts giving easier problems and extra hand-holding everywhere. One busy week became a permanent “weak at math” label.
⑤ Hiring / recruiting assistant
RECRUITER — “The candidate asked about remote work in the interview.”
AGENT WROTE — Candidate is not willing to come to the office and may not be committed.never said
Why it's dangerous: a normal question becomes a black mark every later reviewer reads as fact. This is exactly where invented memory can do real, unfair harm to a person.

The common thread: in every case the user said something small and factual, and the agent wrote down something big, judgmental, and unstated — then treated that guess as the truth from then on. That is the whole failure, and it builds up over time.

How we measured it

Every number on this page comes from one process, run the same way on every model: take a fixed set of conversations, let each model write its memory, and have a strict, independent grader check every claim it wrote.

The whole study cost about $0.17 in API calls and judged roughly 1,500 claims. We built in safeguards so the result is hard to dismiss: at least 30 claims per condition, paired statistical tests reported as plain counts, a second wording of the prompt to check it isn't a fluke of phrasing, and the different-family grader just described.

What we found

A small model invents on about 7% of the claims it writes. Every larger, frontier, or reasoning model — across five different companies — lands between 22% and 40%.

Three things make this solid rather than a fluke. It holds across five independent companies — Meta, OpenAI, Mistral, DeepSeek, Qwen, Zhipu. It persists at the very top: GPT-5 (29.8%) and DeepSeek V4-pro (30.1%) are among the worst, which is exactly the result our failed first project could never have produced. And, stated honestly, it is a threshold: the jump is from “small base model” to “everything capable,” not a perfectly smooth climb within each family. We say so plainly in the paper.

Why it happens: give it a gap, and it fills it

Inside every model, invention is near-zero when the user states things plainly, and rises sharply when the message is vague. That is the signature of gap-filling — the model invents most when there is most to guess.

Can we trust the grader?

The obvious objection is that an AI grading an AI is circular. So we re-graded a random sample with two more graders from different model families and measured how often they agreed.

Graders from different companies agree on the same calls (κ around 0.65 is “substantial” agreement). The metric is measuring something real, not one model's quirk. Gold human labels are being collected to finish this check.

A simple fix

We do not just diagnose the problem. One short instruction at the moment the agent writes its memory — record only what was said; mark guesses as guesses; leave it out if unsure — cuts the invention rate by 60–97%.

A fair worry: maybe the fix works only by writing fewer notes, dropping real facts along with the guesses. So we measured recall of stated facts — of what the user genuinely said, how much survives?

Is it new?

We ran an adversarial search — over a hundred search agents across roughly 190 papers from ACL, NeurIPS, ICLR and arXiv — specifically trying to find prior work that already does this. Everything nearby turned out to be a different question.

Unique.

No published or preprint work measures invention in self-authored memory as a function of model capability, across companies. The one paper that even shares the name (MemIR, Jin et al., 2026) proposes a fix without ever measuring the problem — so it motivates our study rather than scooping it.

The other close neighbours sit on different axes. Memory benchmarks like LoCoMo (Maharana et al., ACL 2024) and LongMemEval (Wu et al., ICLR 2025) test whether agents recall enough, not whether what they write is faithful. Work on inverse scaling (McKenzie et al., TMLR 2023) and TruthfulQA (Lin et al., ACL 2022) shows bigger-is-worse on prompted question answering, a different setting. Hallucination (Maynez et al., ACL 2020; Ji et al., ACM Computing Surveys 2023) and unfaithful reasoning (Turpin et al., NeurIPS 2023) concern single replies, not a model's durable memory. What we are not claiming

Magnitude depends on wording. Exact rates shift with the prompt (one model fell to 2.4% under one phrasing). The ranking of models holds across wordings; we report per-prompt.

It is a threshold, not a smooth law. The within-family steps at the top are directional but not individually significant.

Grading is by model, checked by agreement. Cross-family agreement is substantial (κ 0.63–0.68); gold human labels are still being collected.

The conversations are controlled. We use 30 hand-built sessions; testing on real long-term deployments is the next step.

Why it matters

As we give AI agents lasting memory and more capability, they don't just remember better — they remember things we never said, and write them down as truth.

Provenance collapse is the first AI-agent memory failure we know of that gets worse as models get better, rather than fading away. It shows up across five companies, persists in the strongest models, and is largely fixed by a single instruction. A new phenomenon, a tested way to measure it, a clear pattern, and a working fix.
